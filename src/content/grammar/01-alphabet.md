## The Greek Alphabet

The Greek alphabet has 24 letters, divided into uppercase (majuscule) and lowercase (minuscule) forms. Learning to recognize and pronounce these letters is your first step toward reading the Greek New Testament.

### Special Notes on Pronunciation

**Vowels and Diphthongs:**
- **α (alpha)** = short "a" (as in "cat")
- **ε (epsilon)** = short "e" (as in "pet")
- **η (eta)** = long "e" (as in "say")
- **ι (iota)** = long "i" (as in "machine")
- **ο (omicron)** = short "o" (as in "stop")
- **υ (upsilon)** = "oo" (as in "boot")
- **ω (omega)** = long "o" (as in "phone")

**Consonants with Special Pronunciation:**
- **γ (gamma)** = before γ, κ, χ, ξ, it's pronounced as "ng"
- **θ (theta)** = "th" (as in "think")
- **φ (phi)** = "ph" (as in "phone")
- **χ (chi)** = "kh" (harder than "ch" in "church")
- **ψ (psi)** = "ps" (as in "psychology")

### Sigma (Σ/σ/ς)

Sigma has three forms:
- Σ = capital letter
- σ = lowercase letter at the beginning or middle of a word
- ς = lowercase letter at the **end** of a word only

All three are pronounced the same ("s").

### Practice Tips

1. **Write them out** — Copy the Greek alphabet multiple times by hand
2. **Say them aloud** — Hear yourself pronouncing each letter
3. **Read real examples** — Start with short Greek words from the NT
4. **Recognize patterns** — Greek letters appear frequently in mathematics and science

The letters you'll encounter most frequently in the NT are: α, β, δ, ε, γ, η, θ, ι, κ, λ, μ, ν, ο, π, ρ, σ/ς, τ, υ, φ, χ, ω.
