## Third Declension Overview

The third declension includes all remaining nouns, mostly **consonant-stem nouns**. These are more complex than the first two declensions but very important.

### Characteristics

- **Mixed genders** (masculine, feminine, neuter)
- **Complex stem changes** in oblique cases
- **Diverse nominative endings** (-ς, -ξ, -ψ, -ν, etc.)
- **Common and important words** (σάρξ, πνεύμα, βασιλεύς, etc.)

### Common Third Declension Patterns

**Consonant-final stems:**
- σάρξ (flesh) — stem σαρκ- + nominative -ς = σάρξ
- νύξ (night) — stem νυκ- + nominative -ς = νύξ

**Stop consonants (γ, δ, β, κ, τ, π):**
- When the nominative ending -ς is added to a stop, it combines with it
- Dental consonant (τ/δ) + ς = ς (drops the consonant)
- Guttural consonant (κ/γ) + ς = ξ
- Labial consonant (π/β) + ς = ψ

### Stem Identification

To find the stem of a third declension noun, **look at the genitive singular**:
- σάρξ (nom. sg.) → σαρκός (gen. sg.) — stem is σαρκ-
- βασιλεύς (nom. sg.) → βασιλέως (gen. sg.) — stem is βασιλε(ϝ)-
- πνεῦμα (nom. sg.) → πνεύματος (gen. sg.) — stem is πνευμ-

### Nominative Plural Forms

Third declension has various nominative plural endings:
- **-ες** (most common): σάρκες (fleshes), παῖδες (children)
- **-α** (neuter): πνεύματα (spirits), σώματα (bodies)
- **-εις**: βασιλεῖς (kings), πολῖται (citizens)

### Special Features

**Neuter third declension:**
- Nominative and accusative are always identical
- Nominative plural ends in -α: πνεύματα

**Feminine third declension:**
- σάρξ (flesh) — nominative plural σάρκες
- Most have persistent accents

### Example: Matthew 26:41 (The Spirit is Willing)

**τὸ πνεῦμα πρόθυμον, ἡ σὰρξ ἀσθενής**

- τὸ πνεῦμα = nominative neuter singular (subject, "the spirit")
- ἡ σάρξ = nominative feminine singular (subject, "the flesh")
- Shows gender and number clearly marked

### Declension Pattern

Third declension nouns vary significantly. Learn by **recognition** rather than memorization:
1. Identify the stem (from genitive)
2. Recognize the pattern
3. Familiar words become automatic

This is a complex declension, but the most important rule is: **the stem remains constant throughout, only the ending changes**.
