## Accents in Greek

Greek uses three accent marks placed above vowels to indicate stress and intonation. These are essential for proper pronunciation and can sometimes change the meaning of a word.

### The Three Accents

1. **Acute Accent (΄)** — represents a higher pitch on the syllable (most common)
   - Example: λόγος (lóg-os) — the word

2. **Grave Accent (`)** — represents a lower pitch (only on the final syllable when before another word)
   - Example: ὁ λόγος (hò lóg-os) — the word (with grave on final syllable)

3. **Circumflex Accent (῀)** — represents a falling pitch on a long syllable
   - Example: ἀγάπη (a-gáp-ay) — love
   - Example: ψυχῆ (psy-khe) — soul (circumflex on final long syllable)

### Accent Rules

**Oxytone words** (accent on final syllable):
- λόγος (word) — must have acute accent

**Paroxytone words** (accent on second-to-last syllable):
- ἄγγελος (angel) — keeps acute accent even when others precede

**Proparoxytone words** (accent on third-from-last syllable):
- ἄγγελος can become ἀγγέλου (genitive) — accent moves to new penultimate

The accent cannot appear more than three syllables from the end of a word.

## Breathing Marks (Spiritus)

Every word beginning with a vowel or ρ (rho) has a breathing mark placed above it.

### The Two Breathing Marks

1. **Smooth Breathing (ἀ)** — no initial "h" sound
   - Example: ἀγάπη (agáp-ay) — no "h" before the "a"

2. **Rough Breathing (ἁ)** — represents an initial "h" sound
   - Example: ἡμέρα (h-ay-mér-ah) — "h" before the "ay"

**Special Rule:** Rho (ρ) always has a rough breathing at the beginning of a word:
- ῥήματα (hray-mah-tah) — words

### Diphthongs and Breathing Marks

When a diphthong (two vowels together) has a breathing mark, it appears on the first vowel:
- αἱ (hai) — with rough breathing
- αὐτός (haf-tos) — self

When the second vowel of a diphthong is subscript (smaller, written below), breathing goes on the first vowel:
- ᾳ, ῃ, ῳ — alpha, eta, omega with subscript iota

## Punctuation Marks

Greek uses three punctuation marks:

1. **Period (.)** — marks the end of a sentence
   - λόγος ἦν.

2. **Comma (,)** — marks a clause division (used similarly to English)
   - τὸ λόγος ἦν, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν.

3. **Question Mark (;)** — the Greek question mark looks like a semicolon!
   - Τί ἐστιν αὐτό; — What is this?

**Upper Dot (·)** — Also used in older manuscripts to separate phrases (like a high period)

## Practice Examples from John 3:16

**Ἱνα** — begins with smooth breathing on iota, acute on second syllable
- Pronounced: "hee-nah" (in order that)

**ἠγάπησεν** — rough breathing on eta, acute on second syllable
- Pronounced: "hay-gah-pay-sen" (he loved)

**κόσμος** — acute on first syllable, smooth breathing on omicron
- Pronounced: "kós-mos" (world)

## Remember

- **Accents affect pronunciation** — they indicate which syllable to stress
- **Breathing marks matter** — smooth = no "h", rough = "h"
- **These modify vowels**, not consonants
- In modern Biblical Greek, accents are often not strictly observed in speech, but they remain important for **written recognition** and understanding word relationships
