## Second Declension Overview

The second declension includes:
- **Masculine nouns ending in -ος**
- **Neuter nouns ending in -ον**

These are among the most frequent nouns in the Greek New Testament.

### Masculine -ος Nouns (λόγος — word)

See the paradigm table above for λόγος.

**Key features:**
- **All masculine** (article is always ὁ)
- **Nominative singular: -ος**
- **Nominative plural: -οι**
- **Stem remains constant: λόγ-**
- **Genitive singular: -ου**
- **Neuter rule doesn't apply** (it's masculine)

### Neuter -ον Nouns (ἔργον — work)

See the paradigm table above for ἔργον.

**Key features:**
- **All neuter** (article is always τό)
- **Nominative singular: -ον**
- **Nominative plural: -α** (characteristic neuter plural)
- **Nominative and accusative are always identical** (neuter rule)
- **Genitive singular: -ου** (same as masculine)

### The Neuter Rule

**All neuter nouns in ALL declensions follow this rule:**
- Nominative = Accusative in all numbers
- Nominative/accusative plural always ends in **-α** (or -η)

This is crucial for identifying the function of neuter nouns in sentences!

### Examples from John 1:14

**Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν θεός.**

- ὁ λόγος = nominative masculine (subject)
- τὸν θεόν = accusative neuter (but θεός is usually masculine context)
- πρὸς τὸν θεόν = "toward God" (accusative after preposition)

### Accent Persistence

Most second declension nouns keep their accent on the same syllable:
- λόγος → λόγου → λόγῳ (accent stays on the stem)
- ἔργον → ἔργου → ἔργῳ (accent persists)

Some masculine forms show circumflex on the genitive and dative:
- ὁ δοῦλος, τοῦ δούλου, τῷ δούλῳ

### Practice Exercise

Decline these second declension nouns in all cases, singular and plural:
1. ὁ λόγος (word)
2. τὸ ἔργον (work)
3. τὸ ἵππον (horse) — note the -ον ending

The second declension represents perhaps 30-40% of all nouns in the NT. Mastery here is essential!
